Map projections

Map projections are a method for representing the surface of the Earth (or Earth-like objects) on a flat surface.

Flattening a 3D sphere onto a 2D surface inevitably leads to some distortion. Different projections distort in different ways.

The ideal map projection is therefore the one with the most acceptable type of distortion for your use-case.

Equal-area projections

When the area of a shape in real life matches the area of that shape on the projected map then the projection is called an equal-area projection.

Conformal projections

When angles in real-life match the angles on the projected map then the projection is called a conformal projection.

For example, if two roads cross each other at a 39° angle, then their images on a map with a conformal projection cross at a 39° angle.1

The equirectangular projection

This simple projection is almost 2000 years old.

Each line of meridian (line of longitude) is mapped to vertical straight lines with constant spacing.

This projection isn’t equal-area or conformal, so isn’t very useful for navigation, but can be used for thematic maps, e.g. showing population density or surface temperature.

Mercator projection

Invented in 1569 by Flemish cartographer Gerardus Mercator.

Became popular with sailors because it represents north as up and south as down on all parts of the map while preserving local directions and shapes.

One major downside of this projection is it artificially inflates objects the further they are from the equator. On a mercator map Greenland looks about the same size as Africa, but Africa is actually 14x larger than Greenland.
